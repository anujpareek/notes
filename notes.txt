To ask before building something for customer I consult with: The observation is simple:

Just because a solution works doesn't mean anyone will use it.
Russell spent decades on manufacturing shop floors. He described watching technology get introduced to workers who had no say in the design — and no reason to trust it would make their jobs better rather than harder. Adoption failed. Not because the technology was bad. Because nobody asked the people doing the work what they actually needed.
A colleague of mine put it a different way recently: stand beside your client and observe what they do before you demo anything. Understand how they actually work before you propose how they could work differently.
As technologists, we fall in love with our solutions. We can see so clearly how something could work that we skip the step of asking whether it will work — for this person, in this environment, solving this specific problem.
Capability is not adoption. A tool that works but doesn't get used is just expensive shelf art.
The most sophisticated AI implementation I've ever seen fail wasn't a technical failure. It was a trust failure. Nobody brought the end users along. Nobody asked what friction they were actually trying to eliminate. The solution solved a problem the builders had — not the problem the users had.
The difference between a solution that works and a solution that gets used is almost never the technology.
It's whether the people it was built for feel like it was built for them.
What's the most important question to ask before building any solution?
